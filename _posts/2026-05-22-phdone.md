I'm leaving Berkeley soon.
I want to share some fun moments at Berkeley.

## (pre-)First year

So I started my PhD in Fall 2018. Now it is Spring 2026, and you'll see soon why it took me so long (you can guess if you're Korean).
Until my third year in undergraduate, I hadn't thought about studying abroad. The major event that changed my mind was the [Fields Undergraduate Summer Research Program](https://www.fields.utoronto.ca/generalinfo/Fields-Undergraduate-Summer-Research-Program), which is a summer REU at the Fields Institute in Toronto. I attended it when I was a senior, and it made me realize that I might be better off if I studied abroad and met more people in the world.
But it was a bit late to apply for PhD programs, so I decided to do a master's first at POSTECH.
I applied to 15 schools (I wanted to apply to more, maybe around 30, but my English was poor and so was my TOEFL score) and got into Berkeley. The admission email ended up in my spam folder and I found it a week after it arrived (so anyone applying to PhD programs or anything else — *please check your spam folder*).

I really enjoyed my first year. I took some courses and learned a lot (though I didn't do so well).
My favorite class was David Nadler's Lie Groups course, where he explained the subject in a very intuitive and geometric way. The exams were memorable too; they were all short-answer questions, and one of them gave us a list of 10 Lie groups and asked us to group them by which ones share the same Lie algebra (you can find it [here](https://math.berkeley.edu/~nadler/2018.math261a.midterm2.pdf)).
But more importantly, all my cohort-mates were really kind and very passionate about mathematics. Sometimes we would talk about math endlessly, and I was really happy about that. Also, Berkeley is close to San Francisco, so I could attend a lot of concerts, which was good for my mental health.
I once even went to a Muse concert with a bunch of my cohorts.

## Alternative Military Service, Covid, etc

Unfortunately, I had to go back to Korea for alternative military service in 2019.
Since I didn't want to think about the qualifying exam after coming back 3 years later, I took it at the end of my first year. Berkeley's QE is an oral exam where students choose 3 topics (two major and one minor), and I chose Automorphic Forms, Number Theory, and Complex Analysis (Minor). I passed the exam, although I think I didn't do so well in Automorphic Forms. You can find my QE syllabus and questions [here](https://seewoo5.github.io/assets/Qual_report.pdf).

After I came back to Korea, I started looking for a company to work at. For context, I (like many other people) had two choices: go into active military service (1.5 years), or work at a government-approved company for alternative military service (3 years).
The first is much shorter, but people often prefer the second because they can do something more interesting.
I took the second path and eventually got a job at [Riiid](https://corp.socra.ai/), an ed-tech company that uses AI to help students learn better (the company has since changed its name for several reasons that I don't know). I worked there for almost two years, developing deep learning models for student performance prediction (usually called "knowledge tracing" in the literature) and recommendation systems.
It was actually my first time learning about deep learning, and I picked up a lot. It was also the era when BERT, GPT-2, Reformer, and others came out, and people were amazed.
At the time, it was even possible to search all papers accepted to ICML that used transformers and read them all — at least the abstracts, [which I did](https://seewoo5.tistory.com/25) — but now that simply doesn't make sense because every paper uses transformers.
I also learned about reinforcement learning and [Adam Wagner's work](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.14516) on finding counterexamples to conjectures in graph theory using RL, which was the first paper I read on "AI for Mathematics."

I then moved to another company, [CryptoLab](https://www.cryptolab.co.kr/en/home/). It is a very different kind of company, working on homomorphic encryption and applying it to private machine learning.
I decided to move there because it seemed more mathematical, especially given how homomorphic encryption (or lattice-based cryptography) is related to number theory.
Eventually, I worked on developing a Python library on top of our homomorphic encryption library (called HEAAN, based on the CKKS scheme), which turned out to be mostly algorithm design work.
In short, you have a limited set of operations on vectors of fixed length (component-wise addition and multiplication, and rotation), and you want to implement ML algorithms using as few operations as possible (especially multiplications and rotations).
In some sense, I could do this without knowing much about the CKKS scheme itself, and luckily I was able to write [a paper](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v202/lee23m/lee23m.pdf) based on the work[^1].
 (my only paper on major(?) AI conference, also Oral, although I think we were very lucky).

All of this happened from 2019 to 2022, which was also when the Covid-19 pandemic started.
Sometimes I had to work from home, and it was not much fun since I couldn't meet my colleagues in person.
But anyway, things got better and 3 years passed.
The worst thing was that all the great gigs and concerts in Korea were cancelled.

## Back to Berkeley, Strike, Thesis

I came back to Berkeley in Fall 2022.
Now all my cohorts were in their 5th year, and I was the only one in my 2nd year.
The department was full of strangers and honestly it was pretty lonely at first. 
Since I didn't take any courses as others after coming back, I only attended seminars and mostly talked to the undergrads in my discussion sections.
Since it was pre-ChatGPT, many students come to the sections and asked a lot of questions, and I really enjoyed talking to them.
BUT! there was a [strike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_University_of_California_academic_workers%27_strike) for the semsester, where graduate students and postdocs went on strike for better pay and working conditions. It lasted for about two months, and ended up with (much better) pay and a lot of other benefits for the students, but I was not able to finish my teaching and talk to the students.
However, I faced some of them (to be precise, three of them) quite a lot for the remaining years, which was fun.
Also my friends found [this](https://www.reddit.com/r/berkeley/comments/11pxd1g/who_are_the_math_gsis_that_are_currently_carrying/) and sent it to me, which might be the best achievement of my graduate student life.

I also had to decide on a thesis topic.
[My advisor](https://math.berkeley.edu/~swshin/) suggested me to read Getz-Hahn's [GTM 300 book](https://sites.duke.edu/jgetz/graduate-text/) on automorphic forms and representations and try to find something interesting from it, so I did and found one topic: covering groups and relative trace formula (the book itself has a subtitle "with a view toward Trace Formulae", and actually it is toward *Relative* Trace Formulae).
At some point, my interest became Ichino-Ikeda type conjectures for covering groups exceptional groups.
In particular, I read all four Bhargava's famous papers on higher composition laws, since they are closely related to automorphic forms on exceptional groups (more precisely, they become indexing sets of Fourier coefficients - for example, Bhargava's cube is closely related to automorphic forms on $D_4$ modular forms). Although I don't actively working on it now, I still have a long list of related problems, and I hope to work on them in the future.
(Maybe some of them can be one-shotted by frontier LLMs...)

## Sphere packing, informally

One of the biggest moment in my (math) life was Dan Romik's talk on his new proof of (quasi)modular form inequalities appear in Viazovska's paper on 8-dimensional sphere packing.
I described the story in the introduction of my [thesis](https://seewoo5.github.io/assets/thesis.pdf), so let me make it short here.
In Viazovska's paper, she constructed an (actually, the) optimal function for Cohn-Elkies bound for dimension 8 using (quasi)modular forms, and the proof reduces to proving inequalities of certain quasimodular forms.
She used interval arithmetic, and Romik found another proof using several modular form identities and explicit values of them at $z = i$ (special case of Chowla-Selberg formula).
He left dimension 24 cases as an open problem, especially for graduate students, and I decided to give it a try.
This was more interesting and harder than I expected, and I eventually [finished](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.14659) in Summer of 2024.
At this point, I found that there are a lot of problems related to this, or more generally positivity of quasimodular forms, and I decided to work on them for my thesis.
If you are interested, you can find the details in my thesis - for now, there's [another paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.10536) I uploaded on arXiv, and probably two more (which are both incomplete in some sense, so I need to spend more time).

## Sphere packing, formally

After I uploaded my paper on arXiv, Yaël Dillies found it and shared on the Xena Discord server.

As a coincidence, [Sidharth Hariharan](https://thefundamentaltheor3m.github.io/) was also in the server, and he just started a project on formalizing Viazovska's proof of the 8-dimensional sphere packing problem in Lean while he was an exchange student at EPFL.
So he reached out to me, and I joined the project, along with Chris Birkbeck, Gareth Ma, and Bhavik Mehta, which was private at the moment.
Before we made it public at the Big Proof conference, we write some basic definitions and the statements of main theorems, and also polished the blueprint.
After it became public, many people started to contribute, and many *AI*s also start to contribute.
And the project got famous [for a reason that we didn't expect](https://www.math.inc/sphere-packing), which also [initiated a discussion about the role of AI in formalization](https://leanprover.zulipchat.com/#narrow/channel/113488-general/topic/The.20role.20of.20AI.20companies.20in.20large.20formalisation.20projects/near/580073247).
There are too many things to say about this, but I won't repeat here, and I recommend to check out [Avigad's essay](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.03684) and also [DeDeo and Duede's paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.13680).[^2]
The only thing I want to repeat and emphasize is: *the project is not finished yet, and we (maintainers) welcome all the contributions* (although the progress get slower than before).
You can find the project repository [here](https://github.com/thefundamentaltheor3m/Sphere-Packing-Lean), and also a summary of the current state of the project [in this paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.23468).


## Last semester

Spring 2026 was my last semester, and also the busiest semester of my life, since I need to write my thesis, teach, and also do internship at [Axiom](https://axiommath.ai/).
I tried hard to find a way not to teach this semester and focusing on the other two, but I failed to do so and ended up doing all these things and got extremely busy.
Overall, I fairly enjoyed working at Axiom, especially working with great colleagues including Ken Ono, Jujian Zhang, and Kenny Lau.
The experience also made me think more throughly about the role of AI in mathematics, which I'd write a blog post about in the future (I already wrote [one](https://seewoo5.github.io/jekyll/update/2026/03/05/early-mathematician-ai.html) for young mathematicians, but maybe one more...).

It was also a semester which made me less interested in teaching, since many of the students were using LLM for learning (and of course doing homework), and the number of students coming to sections was very small (I thought in the exact same way as I did in 2022 for the same course, Math 53, where the attendance rate was 40%-ish. This semester it was less than 10%.)
One day when there were only 4 students (out of 40), I actually asked them what makes to coming to the section even if others use ChatGPT to learn, and our conclusion was that human interaction is still important for them.[^3]

When my internship and teaching duties ended, I had to focus on writing my thesis.
At this point, ChatGPT and Claude helped me A LOT, especially for fixing my poor English but also finding some mathematical errors.
But most importantly, I (or everyone who graduate this semester) had to meet the accessibility requirement for the thesis, i.e. make the pdf file more accessible by adding metadata or taggings.
I learned that it is very hard to do this in TeX (other departments write thesis in MS Word), and every single math graduate students graduating this semester had the same problem.
Eventually, I used Codex and Claude for days to add some TeX options that I don't know anything about, but it resolved all the issues flagged by Acrobat Reader's accessibility checker, and I was able to submit my thesis successfully.
Thank you AI!
